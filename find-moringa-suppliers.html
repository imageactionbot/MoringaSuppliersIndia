Broker red flags (save this list)

Avoid operators who refuse to name the packing line, who ship samples from a mystery city, or who bounce you between WhatsApp numbers mid-negotiation. Serious mills have named QC contacts, bank details matching entity documents, and traceable lot IDs.

If someone insists a generic “EU-certified” PDF is enough without scope lines matching your SKU, escalate diligence—not price negotiation.

Also watch suppliers who will not share insurance certificates for cargo or who change billing entities between PI and shipment without written novation—that pattern correlates with subcontracted lots you never approved.

After you shortlist three suppliers

Run identical RFQs: same SKU wording, same micro panel ask, same destination port. Divergence in answer quality often predicts who survives first arrival QC.

Case notes: RFQ ghosts we see in 2026

Composite A — the jet-lag broker. Promises CIF Hamburg in twelve hours, sends a COA from a lab three states away from the mill, changes packing address mid-week. Resolution: demand video walk-through of the line that will run your lot, then hold pay milestones.

Composite B — the genuine toller. Owns dryers but borrows mill hours; answers slowly yet sends retain photos tied to batch stickers. Often workable if contracts recognise toll path and micro responsibility splits.

Composite C — the catalogue farm. Beautiful photography, vague moisture numbers. Fix: ask for two consecutive weeks of in-house moisture logs—even redacted—before you fly.

What to request in a “verification pack”

Serious buyers ask for: (1) company registration + GST + IEC PDFs with the same name chain, (2) factory photos with dated batch stickers visible, (3) two historical COAs for adjacent SKUs if the exact SKU is new, (4) list of major export destinations with referee contacts if shareable, (5) written deviation policy for micro edge cases. Factories that treat this as insult were never going to survive arrival QA.

Site visits without theatre

If you fly to India, schedule mill floor time before lunch when operators are less performative. Ask to see rejects bin, not just finished pallets. Bring a handheld moisture meter only if you harmonise method with lab—otherwise you create needless fights.

Combine trips with contact protocol so meetings map to real batch windows rather than showroom cookies.

Reference calls that actually work

Ask importers not “is supplier good?” but “what broke in month four, and how did they fix it?” Good suppliers have failure stories with receipts; only marketers claim perfection. If references are mysteriously unavailable, weight that heavily—even in commodity markets.

Contract clauses to discuss early

Cover: force majeure with sensible disease/climate language, title transfer aligned to milestone payments, dispute venue (often Singapore arbitration for Asia-EU pairs), and non-conforming goods destruction or return mechanics. Lawyers earn their fees preventing six-figure ambiguities—you are not rude by raising clauses before tasting tea at the factory.

OSINT hygiene (light touch)

Quick scans: GSTIN active status, litigation news in registrar portals, and whether the factory address matches geotagged photos. None replaces site visit—but filters obvious burner operations before you spend visa budget.

Save screenshots with dates when you discover green flags too—serious factories appreciate buyers who document diligence without theatre.
